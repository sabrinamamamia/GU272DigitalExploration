In 1634, the first group of Jesuits settled in Maryland. The Jesuits received land grants to manage farms that indentured servants worked on. As the tobacco economy grew, indentured workers were increasingly replaced by enslaved Africans. By the 19th century, the Jesuits became one of the largest landlords and slaveowners in Maryland.

In 1789, Georgetown University was founded by John Carroll, the first Catholic bishop in the United States. Many of Georgetown's early directors and presidents owned enslaved Africans on Maryland plantations. Four of these plantations were White Marsh, St. Thomas, Newtown, and St. Inigoes.

In June 1838, Georgetown president and Jesuit priest Fr. Thomas Mulledy agreed to sell 272 enslaved men, women, and children to pay off the university's debts. The buyers were Henry Johnson, a member of the House of Representatives, and Jesse Beatty, a landowner in Louisiana. The enslaved people would be sent to Louisiana to work on sugar plantations. The sale was worth about $3.3 million in today's dollars.

In Dec. 1838, a group of the enslaved individuals were transported from Maryland to Louisiana on the ship manifest of the Katharine Jackson. The cargo included dozens of children, among them infants as young as 2 months old. They were shipped by Robert A. Windsor of Alexandria and consigned to Lambeth and Thompson of New Orleans.
